How a VPN Protects Your Business and Privacy.

Every time you connect to the internet, you leave a digital trail. Your IP address reveals your approximate location. Your browsing activity can be monitored by your internet service provider. And if you're on public Wi‑Fi—at a hotel, airport, or café—your login credentials, emails, and financial information could be exposed to anyone with basic hacking tools sitting on the same network.

Most people don't think about this until something goes wrong: a compromised bank account, stolen login credentials, or a suspicious charge. Everyday internet use carries real risks that many of us underestimate.

This Affiliate Partner Spotlight will review VPNs—and spotlight NordVPN as a trusted solution for both personal and business use. Whether you work from home, manage a team, or simply want safer browsing, this spotlight is for you.

What Is a VPN? A Plain-Language Explanation

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) is software that creates an encrypted connection between your device and the internet. When you turn on a VPN, your data travels through a secure, private "tunnel" instead of flowing openly across your network.

Without a VPN, your internet activity is like sending postcards, anyone handling them along the way can read the message. With a VPN, your activity is sealed inside an envelope that only you and the intended recipient can open.

Two key things happen when you use a VPN:

  • Your data is encrypted, making it unreadable to anyone who intercepts it
  • Your IP address is masked, hiding your real location

VPNs Used in Businesses

Businesses handle sensitive information such as client records, payment data, internal communications, proprietary strategies. A single security breach can cost thousands of dollars and devastate customer trust.

Real-World Business Example: The Traveling Sales Team

Imagine a sales team that travels weekly. They connect to hotel Wi‑Fi to check the company CRM, send proposals, and process invoices. Without a VPN, every one of those sessions is a potential exposure point. A hacker on the same hotel network could intercept login credentials in minutes using freely available tools.

With a VPN, that same sales rep's traffic is encrypted end-to-end. Even if someone intercepts the data packets, they see only scrambled, useless information. Businesses rely on VPNs to:

  • Protect employee access to cloud tools, email, and internal systems
  • Secure data transfers when teams work remotely or travel
  • Reduce risk of credential theft on untrusted networks
  • Meet compliance and data protection expectations

VPNs Used in Home Offices

The shift to remote work has turned millions of homes into offices. Home networks were never designed with enterprise-level security in mind. Your home Wi‑Fi likely shares bandwidth with smart TVs, children's tablets, gaming consoles, and guest devices, each one a potential vulnerability.

Real-World Home Office Example: The Freelance Accountant

Consider a freelance accountant who manages tax filings and financial records for dozens of clients. They work from a home office and occasionally from a co‑working space. Without a VPN, every file upload, every client portal login, and every email attachment travels across the open internet or a shared network.

If that accountant's connection is intercepted, client Social Security numbers, bank details, and income records could be exposed. A VPN encrypts all that traffic, making interception practically useless to an attacker. Home office users rely on VPNs to:

  • Encrypt sensitive client and business data on residential networks
  • Protect logins when switching between home and public Wi‑Fi
  • Add a professional-grade security layer without complex IT infrastructure

How Hiding Your IP Address and Location Protects You

Your IP address is like a digital home address. It reveals your approximate geographic location, your internet service provider, and combined with other data can be used to build a profile of your online activity. Here's why masking it matters.

Preventing Location-Based Targeting

When your IP address is visible, websites, advertisers, and even malicious actors can determine your city or region. This information can be used to:

  • Serve you targeted ads based on where you live
  • Adjust prices dynamically (some sites charge more based on location)
  • Identify your physical area for social engineering or phishing attacks

A VPN replaces your real IP with the VPN server's IP, making your actual location unknown to the sites you visit.

Stopping Network-Level Surveillance

Your internet service provider (ISP) can see every website you visit and every service you connect to. In some countries, ISPs are legally allowed to sell browsing data to advertisers. A VPN encrypts your traffic so your ISP sees only that you're connected to a VPN, not what you're doing.

Reducing Risk of Personal Attacks

If you leave a product review that upsets someone, post an opinion on a public forum, or engage in online commerce. Without a VPN, a determined person could potentially use your IP address to narrow down your location. With a VPN active, that trail leads to a server in another city or country, not to your doorstep.

Having a VPN Makes You Safer in Everyday Life

Let's walk through a few scenarios most people encounter regularly.

Scenario 1: Checking Your Bank Account at Starbucks

You open your banking app on the café's free Wi‑Fi. Without a VPN, a hacker using a "man-in-the-middle" attack on that network could capture your session data. With a VPN, your banking session is encrypted and invisible to the attacker.

Scenario 2: Shopping Online from a Hotel Room

You're browsing for deals on your phone while staying at a hotel. Without a VPN, your ISP or the hotel's network administrator could see which sites you're visiting. With a VPN, your online activity is hidden, protecting your privacy.

Scenario 3: Working from Home While Your Kids Stream Movies

Your teenager downloads a file that introduces malware onto the shared home network. If a VPN is active on your work device, your business traffic remains encrypted even if the broader network is compromised.

The pattern is clear: a VPN doesn't prevent every threat, but it closes one of the most common and exploitable gaps, unencrypted, visible internet traffic.
